1. Multiplicity sequence

Multiplicity sequence

Definition

Let \(S\) be a numerical semigroup, \(M = S^*\) the maximal ideal of \(S\) and \(\mathcal{B}(M)\) the blowup of \(M\). It is known that \(\mathcal{B}(M)\) is a numerical semigroup such that \(S \subseteq \mathcal{B}(M)\), then, setting \(S^1 = \mathcal{B}(M)\), it can be considered the blowup of its maximal ideal, \(S^2\), and repeating the argument, it is obtained a chain of numerical semigroups

\[ S = S^0 \subseteq S^1 \subseteq S^2 \subseteq \cdots \subseteq S^i \subseteq S^{i+1} \subseteq\cdots \]

where \(S^{i+1}\) is the blowup of the maximal ideal of \(S^i\) for all \(i \in \mathbb{N}\). This chain stabilizes at \(\mathbb{N}\), and if we consider the multiplicity \(m_i = m(S^i)\) of each \(S^i\), we get a non increasing sequence \((m_0, m_1, m_2, \ldots)\) that stabilizes at \(1\). It is defined the multiplicity sequence of \(S\) as the sequence \((m_0, m_1, m_2, \ldots)\).

Examples

\(\circ\) Let \(S = \langle 3, 5, 7 \rangle\). We have \(m(S) = 3\) and \(S^*\) has reduction number \(r(S^*) = 1\), since

\[ 2S^* = S^* + S^* = \{6, 8, \rightarrow \} = 3 + S^* = m(S) + S^*. \] Then, \(S^1 = \mathcal{B}(S^*) = S^* - S^* = \{0, 2, \rightarrow \}\). Now, \(m(S^1) = 2\) and \(r((S^1)^*) = 1\),

\[ 2(S^1)^* = (S^1)^* + (S^1)^* = \{4, \rightarrow \} = 2 + (S^1)^*. \]

Finally, \(S^2 = \mathcal{B}((S^1)^*) = (S^1)^* - (S^1)^* = \mathbb{N}\) and the multiplicity sequence of \(S\) is \((3,2,1)\).

Examples with GAP

The following example is made with the package NumericalSgps in GAP.

\(\diamond\) Let \(S = \langle 12, 17, 21, 30 \rangle\), in GAP:

gap> S := NumericalSemigroup(12, 17, 21, 30);
<Numerical semigroup with 4 generators>

The function MultiplicitySequence returns the multiplicity sequence of a numerical semigroup.

gap> MultiplicitySequence(S);
[ 12, 5, 4, 1 ]

If we are interested in the blowups and the maximal ideals generated in the process, the functions BlowUp and MaximalIdeal returns the blowup of a proper ideal and the maximal ideal of a numerical semigroup, respectively.

gap> M0 := MaximalIdeal(S);
<Ideal of numerical semigroup>
gap> SmallElements(M0);
[ 12, 17, 21, 24, 29, 30, 33, 34, 36, 38, 41, 42, 45, 46, 47, 48, 50, 51, 53, 54, 55, 57, 58, 59, 60, 62, 63, 64, 65,
  66, 67, 68, 69, 70, 71, 72, 74 ]
gap> S1 := BlowUp(M0);
<Ideal of numerical semigroup>
gap> SmallElements(S1);
[ 0, 5, 9, 10, 12, 14, 15, 17 ]
S1 := NumericalSemigroupBySmallElements(SmallElements(S1));
<Numerical semigroup>
gap> M1 := MaximalIdeal(S1);
<Ideal of numerical semigroup>
gap> S2 := BlowUp(M1);
<Ideal of numerical semigroup>
gap> SmallElements(S2);
[ 0, 4, 5, 7 ]
gap> S2 := NumericalSemigroupBySmallElements(SmallElements(S2));
<Numerical semigroup>
gap> M2 := MaximalIdeal(S2);
<Ideal of numerical semigroup>
gap> S3 := BlowUp(M2);
<Ideal of numerical semigroup>
gap> SmallElements(S3);
[ 0 ]

The function SmallElements has been used to show the left elements of the numerical semigroup and NumericalSemigroupBySmallElements have been used to generate the numerical semigroup from the blowup, because the MaximalIdeal function needs a numerical semigroup, not an ideal. As we can see, \(S^1 = \{0, 5, 9, 10, 12, 14, 15, 17, \rightarrow \}, S^2 = \{0, 4, 5, 7, \rightarrow \}\) and \(S^3 = \mathbb{N}\).
